What I Do

Two Worlds.
One Person.

Most people live on one side of the divide — the design table or the job site. Aaron occupies both. Fluent in the language of plumbing engineers and the language of pipefitters with their hands in the pipe. That's rare. That's the value.

The Engineering Side
Working with MEP engineers on building design — what specifications should say, how materials are selected based on temperature, pressure, media, and longevity. Educating engineers on how their language is actually interpreted in the field. Aaron has presented CEU training at ASPE chapters across the Midwest — Chicago, Wisconsin, Michigan, Indiana, St. Louis, Cleveland, and Central Ohio — and nationally on behalf of ASPE. "When you type this in your plans, this is what you're communicating to the rest of the world — is that what you intend?"
The Field Side
Working alongside the contractors, installers, and apprentices with tools in their hands. Teaching hands-on techniques in copper joinery, press fitting, and valve systems. Being the go-to resource when things go sideways on a job. There are real design differences between bronze and brass — how they react to heat, soldering best practices — and Aaron teaches all of it. "The nexus of what I do is when the plumber or pipefitter has our product in their hand. If he or she has success installing it, and can work safely, efficiently, and profitably, and the product works the way it's supposed to, we all win."
From the engineer's spec sheet to the apprentice's first perfect joint — Aaron has been on both ends of every project. That's rare. That's the value.

Aaron Kilburg is the NIBCO, INC. Midwest Commercial Sales Manager and has been with NIBCO for 9 years. Aaron works with MEP engineers to be a resource for valve and press products, as well as generating demand for engineered products with mechanical, plumbing, and fire protection contractors. Aaron's territory includes the states of Illinois, Iowa, Wisconsin, Michigan, Minnesota, Indiana, Missouri, Kentucky, Ohio, and North and South Dakota.

Mr. Kilburg is a graduate of the University of Illinois and resides in Batavia, Illinois with his wife Kathleen and three children.

He has presented CEU training at ASPE chapters across the Midwest, including Chicago, Wisconsin, Eastern and Western Michigan, Central Indiana, Evansville, St. Louis, Cleveland, and Central Ohio. He has also presented nationally to plumbing engineers on behalf of ASPE for virtual learning events. He also teaches at plumbing and pipefitting UA chapters across the Midwest, discussing commercial valves and best practices and techniques for copper joinery, and provides CEU training for plumbers in the states of Wisconsin, Illinois, and Missouri.

Eleven states across the American Midwest — from Iowa on the western edge to Ohio on the eastern edge, and from Minnesota and the Dakotas in the north down to northern Kentucky. It's a lot of ground to cover, which means Aaron has had to get extraordinary at building real relationships quickly.

"What happens is certain areas get busier. I'll have a great meeting that leads to another meeting, and I end up nesting in an area for three to six months because that's where the activity is. If I have a great meeting with you and then don't follow up for six months — forget about it. How strong is our relationship going to be?" So he does the work. Consistently. Personally.

Aaron doesn't get involved in pricing — which in sales, is practically foreign. "I tell people: I build partnerships and I create demand. When I'm trying to build a relationship, I'm selling myself, my team, our company — and then the widget comes last. Because everybody makes widgets."

He will send you to a competitor if they have something he doesn't. He'll walk away from a deal that's not a good fit. "Having the freedom to say 'maybe this isn't right' is a liberating thing. There's no desperation. In a big territory, there's always someone down the street to go talk to."

He once closed a presentation by saying: "If you think I'm full of it, you should not partner with me. That would be a bad idea." Everyone laughed. But they understood exactly why he was different.

An engineer once told Aaron something he's never forgotten: "Everybody has a warranty on their work. An engineer in Illinois has seven years. A contractor is on the hook for one to two years. But in reality — as long as the owner has a memory — that's how long you have to stand behind your products."

Aaron uses that line. Lives by it. "As long as it's out in the world, I need to have some accountability. That changes your mindset of how you're interacting with your customer. And I think if I say that to a customer and it resonates with them — that's someone I want to partner with."

Aaron gives national presentations to the American Society of Plumbing Engineers. He trains groups of 50 union apprentices. He walks into executive boardrooms. He gets nervous every single time. "My armpits were sweating before that online presentation. But once I get into it, I relax."

His wife asks why he gets nervous when he clearly loves it. "I tell her — that nervousness is your super version of you. The most polished, the most energetic, the most charismatic, the most empathetic — everything turned up to 10. You can't be in that state 24 hours a day. But when you need it, the nerves put you there."

Honest Answers
to Real Questions.

Officially: Midwest Commercial Sales Manager for NIBCO, INC. — a national industrial manufacturer. What that actually means: Aaron helps MEP engineers specify the right systems for buildings, helps contractors win and execute the work, and trains the apprentices who install it — all across an 11-state territory.

The short version? He builds partnerships and creates demand. Not sales. Partnerships.

"I've had a lot of people say, 'You're the first guy I've ever talked to who would actually send me to the competition.' My answer: why would I try to steer you to something that's not going to work?"

If Aaron doesn't have what you need, he'll tell you and point you to someone who does. That conversation gets remembered. That's more valuable than a transaction he shouldn't have made.

By showing up in both rooms. Aaron sits in on engineering design sessions to hear real challenges. He goes to union halls and works hands-on with apprentices. He's given national engineering presentations. He's also stood on job sites.

Training & Education

The Kid and
His Father.

At a recent Illinois Plumbing and Heating conference, a young man walked up to Aaron. He looked about 19. He said: "You're the solder guy. You taught us."

Aaron didn't remember him. When you run 50-person apprentice training sessions multiple times a year, across every corner of the Midwest, individual faces blur together. But the young man remembered Aaron. And then something happened that Aaron won't forget.

"He was with his dad. And he walked his dad through the whole technique — everything I'd taught him, months earlier. And I watched his dad's face. And I'm getting goosebumps right now thinking about it."

The training model Aaron and his colleague Sean have developed involves bringing specialized valve equipment directly to union apprentices — the plumbers and pipefitters just starting their careers. They do a baseline attempt first. Ugly. Room to grow. Then they teach — hands-on, collaborative, one person at a time, using their actual names. Then a second attempt.

"By the second attempt, they're doing elite-level work. Best in class. And I always tell them: you're the most important people in this whole process. The nexus of what I do is when the plumber or pipefitter has our product in their hand. If he or she has success installing it, and can work safely, efficiently, and profitably, and the product works the way it's supposed to, we all win. The owner, the design engineer, the contractor, and my team."

Aaron estimates that he and Sean train 500–700 apprentices per year. "We're training an entire generation of tradespeople on how to do this right. That's a legacy I'm proud of."

A legacy measured not in revenues or rankings — but in a 19-year-old showing his dad, at a conference, how to do something exactly right.
