On the Board

"My job on the board is to support the work of the district — to serve students, families, teachers, administration, staff and taxpayers to the best of our ability — so every student enjoys a sense of belonging and has access to the foundational tools they need to be contributing members of society."

"From Doctor to Ditch Digger, everyone must find their way, and everyone has a contribution they can make. The Board's job is to make sure the education they receive in Batavia didn't limit what was possible for them."

My only agenda serving on the BPS101 School Board is serving the community and district to the best of my ability.

School boards have become deeply politicized — and Aaron doesn't pretend that's easy. "I could say sunsets are great, and someone will say, sunrises are way better — what are you talking about? That's just how it feels sometimes." He approaches every colleague with genuine respect, even when consensus is hard to build.

His focus is always trust. "Disagreement and different perspectives are healthy — that's how it should work. But we should all be operating from a level of trust. If you have an opinion, you should be comfortable telling me. I can disagree and still respect you completely."

His father Dean's philosophy, written in a newspaper clip from the 1970s, has never been more relevant: "Washington D.C. isn't going to solve all of our problems. Some have to be solved by people in their own community."

That's the north star for Aaron in Batavia. Not partisan politics. Not national narratives. Real people, real kids, real outcomes — in the community they share.

Community in Action

We're Here to Support You
Through Thick and Thin.

Some residents of Batavia are joining the fight, in their own way, to help local businesses survive a persistent crisis. On the east side of Batavia, Aaron Kilburg and a group of neighbors — who have become closer through their texting group — have been thinking about what they can do.

"COVID is on all of our minds and when Governor Pritzker announced new restrictions, we were saying we want to help restaurants because we know it's going to be really difficult for them to survive this winter."

One of Aaron's neighbors, Geoff Cheesman, had the idea to organize a neighborhood chat: once a week, pick a restaurant and encourage people to order carry-out on that night. On one night alone, six families ordered from Riverside Pizza.

"I started thinking, if this works, what if 25 or 50 neighborhoods across Batavia started doing something like this? That would have a real impact on some of these restaurants."

"I also think that for the morale of those proprietors to know that — hey, we realize this stuff is going on and we're here to support you guys through thick and thin — I just think it would really have a huge benefit for everybody."

Honest Answers
to Real Questions.

His father Dean modeled a life of service — school boards, civic commissions, community organizations. Aaron ran for the BPS101 school board because his father spent a lifetime modeling what service looks like, and because he's passionate about making a positive contribution to his community.

And when Aaron looks at Batavia, he sees kids who deserve better than political theater. "The work of children should not be political. My job is to make sure every single one of them has the foundations for success — wherever they end up."

Trust first. Everything else depends on it. "Disagreement and different perspectives are healthy — it's how things should work. But we should all be operating from a level of trust. If you have a concern, you should feel safe saying it to me. I can totally disagree with you and still respect you."

He's also studying leadership actively — reading, reflecting, seeking feedback. "I'm trying to figure out how to get better. I know where my growth edges are."

Aaron's refreshingly direct about this: "Technology. Organization. I say yes to too much. I get worn down." He goes to everything, takes every meeting, and ends up stretched thin across his professional, volunteer, and family life.

"My wife says I'm not very efficient. And she's right. When I'm working at a high level, I do the small meaningful things — the handwritten note, the timely follow-up. When I'm worn down, those fall away. And that's when I'm not leveraging the work like I should."

Genuine curiosity. "I actually want to know what you're doing. What your problems are. What you're trying to build." That's not a technique — it's just who he is. And people can feel the difference.

"You don't come across as a salesperson. You come across as a genuine human being who cares about other human beings." That's not Aaron's assessment of himself — that's what people tell him. And it might be the rarest thing in any field.

The Origin

The Skinny Kid from Bellevue.

Ask Aaron Kilburg what his self-image is — right now, today, as a senior sales professional and school board leader with an 11-state territory and a track record you can drive past on the expressway — and he'll tell you without hesitation:

"The skinny kid. The last one picked. That's still who I feel like inside."

He was the smallest kid in his class at Bellevue Elementary. He got bullied a bit. Coke bottle glasses. Not getting asked to things. The girls weren't interested. He wasn't getting picked first for basketball — he was the last kid picked, every time.

"It wasn't that bad. I had loving parents. Food on the table. But I can still relate to someone who feels like the outcast." And that's exactly the point. That capacity for empathy — learned young, never lost — is embedded in everything he does professionally and in the community.

There's a Captain America moment Aaron talks about. Before Steve Rogers gets the super serum, Dr. Abraham Erskine explains why he chose the small, weak kid:

Steve: "Why me?"

Erskine: "I'm not looking for a perfect soldier, but a good man. You see, the strong man who has known power all his life may lose respect for that power. But a weak man knows the value of strength. He knows the value of power."

Erskine: "Whatever happens tomorrow, you must promise me one thing. That you will stay who you are. Not a perfect soldier, but a good man."

Steve: "I'll do my best."

When Aaron closes his eyes, he can still see himself at 10 years old — the smallest kid in his class, coke bottle glasses, the last to be picked for a sports team, someone who got picked on and lacked confidence. It drives him every day to work hard and do the right thing for others. He relates to the underdog and outcast more than the homecoming king. And he wouldn't want it any other way.

"I more naturally relate to the underdog than the prom king. And I think that's made me more effective at everything — connecting, teaching, leading. Because the people who are the most receptive are always the ones who haven't been given very much yet."

Why He Serves.

Aaron's father Dean has spent his entire life in service. He served on the Geneva School Board for twelve years — president for eight of them. Before that, back in Iowa, he sat on civic commissions, coached teams, led the Jaycees — an organization for young men who wanted to invest in their communities. He was the state president of Iowa's Jaycees.

There's a newspaper clipping from the 1970s. Post-Nixon, post-Vietnam, Jimmy Carter, energy crisis — one of the most turbulent eras in modern American life. Aaron's father Dean wrote:

"Washington D.C. isn't going to solve all of our problems. Some have to be solved by people in their own community."
