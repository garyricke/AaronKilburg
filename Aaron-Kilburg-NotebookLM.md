## Who Is Aaron Kilburg?

Aaron Kilburg is the NIBCO, INC. Midwest Commercial Sales Manager, based in Batavia, Illinois. He has been with NIBCO for nine years. He covers an eleven-state territory across the American Midwest — Illinois, Iowa, Wisconsin, Michigan, Minnesota, Indiana, Missouri, Kentucky, Ohio, and North and South Dakota.

He is a graduate of the University of Illinois. He lives in Batavia, Illinois with his wife Kathleen and their three children: Emma, Jake, and Lily.

He serves on the Batavia Public School District Board of Education (BPS 101) as Vice President, and has served on that board for five years. He coaches youth sports. He's a father, a volunteer, a public servant, and — in his own words — a Bridge Builder.

## The Central Idea: Two Worlds, One Person

Most professionals live on one side of a divide. They're either at the design table or on the job site. Aaron Kilburg occupies both — fluently.

He speaks the language of mechanical, plumbing, and fire protection engineers: specifications, pressure ratings, temperature tolerances, valve selection, design intent. And he speaks the language of the tradespeople with their hands in the pipe: copper joinery, soldering technique, press fitting, what it's like to be on the 7th floor of a hospital expansion in Chicago, wondering if the product is going to work.

That's rare. That's the value.

In his own words:

> "I speak engineer, and I speak contractor. I can bridge those two worlds, and I can speak authoritatively to both groups."

## The Engineering Side

Aaron works with MEP (mechanical, electrical, plumbing) engineers during building design. His role is to help engineers understand what specifications should say — what materials to select based on temperature, pressure, media, and longevity requirements — and how that language is actually interpreted in the field.

He has presented CEU (continuing education unit) training at ASPE chapters across the Midwest: Chicago, Wisconsin, Michigan (Eastern and Western), Central Indiana, Evansville, St. Louis, Cleveland, and Central Ohio. He has also presented nationally on behalf of ASPE for virtual learning events.

His message to engineers is direct:

> "When you type this in your plans, this is what you're communicating to the rest of the world — is that what you intend?"

---

## The Field Side

Aaron works alongside the contractors, installers, and apprentices who physically build the systems that engineers design. He teaches hands-on techniques in copper joinery, press fitting, and valve systems. He's the go-to resource when things go sideways on a job.

He understands material science at a practical level: the real design differences between bronze and brass, how metals react to heat, soldering best practices for lead-free materials, flux selection. He teaches all of it.

His core philosophy:

> "The nexus of what I do is when the plumber or pipefitter has our product in their hand. If he or she has success installing it, and can work safely, efficiently, and profitably, and the product works the way it's supposed to, we all win. The owner, the design engineer, the contractor, and my team."

## Philosophy: Partnerships Over Pitches

Aaron doesn't get involved in pricing — which in sales is practically foreign. He describes his work this way:

> "I build partnerships and create demand. When I'm trying to build a relationship, I'm selling myself, my team, our company — and then the widget comes last. Because everybody makes widgets."

He will send a customer to a competitor if they have something he doesn't. He'll walk away from a deal that's not a right fit. He says:

> "Having the freedom to say 'maybe this isn't right' is a liberating thing. There's no desperation. In a big territory, there's always someone down the street to go talk to."

He once closed a presentation by saying:

> "If you think I'm full of it, you should not partner with me. That would be a bad idea."

Everyone laughed. But they understood exactly why he was different.

People can feel the difference between someone performing authenticity and someone who is actually authentic. Aaron is the second kind.

> "People can smell a phony and hate a sales pitch. So I'm always my authentic self and I look to build partnerships."

## On Accountability

An engineer once told Aaron something he has never forgotten:

> "Everybody has a warranty on their work. An engineer in Illinois has seven years. A contractor is on the hook for one to two years. But in reality — as long as the owner has a memory — that's how long you have to stand behind your products."

Aaron uses that line. Lives by it.

> "As long as it's out in the world, I need to have some accountability. That changes your mindset of how you're interacting with your customer. And I think if I say that to a customer and it resonates with them — that's someone I want to partner with."

## Training a Generation: The Teaching Story

Aaron and his colleague Sean run hands-on training sessions with union apprentices across the Midwest — plumbers and pipefitters just starting their careers. They train an estimated 500 to 700 apprentices per year.

The training model is simple and powerful. They bring specialized valve equipment directly to the union hall. They do a baseline attempt first. Ugly. Room to grow. Then they teach — hands-on, collaborative, using the apprentices' actual names, one person at a time. Then a second attempt. By the second attempt, the apprentices are doing elite-level work.

> "I always tell them: you're the most important people in this whole process. The nexus of what I do is when the plumber or pipefitter has our product in their hand. If he or she has success installing it, and can work safely, efficiently, and profitably, and the product works the way it's supposed to, we all win. The owner, the design engineer, the contractor, and my team."

Said to a room of union apprentices who weren't used to being called the most important thing to anyone.

### The Father and Son Moment

At a recent Illinois Plumbing and Heating conference, a young man walked up to Aaron. He looked about 19. He said: *"You're the solder guy. You taught us."*

Aaron didn't remember him. When you run 50-person apprentice training sessions multiple times a year, across every corner of the Midwest, individual faces blur together. But the young man remembered Aaron. And then something happened that Aaron won't forget.

> "He was with his dad. And he walked his dad through the whole technique — everything I'd taught him, months earlier. And I watched his dad's face. And I'm getting goosebumps right now thinking about it."

A legacy measured not in revenues or rankings — but in a 19-year-old showing his dad, at a conference, how to do something exactly right.

### The Lead-Free Law Transition

Material laws changed about eleven years ago — lead-free solder requirements took effect. Techniques changed. Best practices changed. Someone had to bridge that knowledge gap for the entire industry. Aaron and his team did. The training sessions that followed helped an entire generation of tradespeople understand how to work correctly with new materials.

## The Origin Story: The Skinny Kid from Bellevue

Ask Aaron Kilburg what his self-image is — right now, today, as a senior sales professional and school board leader with an 11-state territory — and he'll tell you without hesitation:

> "The skinny kid. The last one picked. That's still who I feel like inside."

He was the smallest kid in his class at Bellevue Elementary. He got bullied a bit. Coke bottle glasses. Not getting asked to things. The girls weren't interested. He was the last kid picked for basketball, every time.

> "It wasn't that bad. I had loving parents. Food on the table. But I can still relate to someone who feels like the outcast."

That capacity for empathy — learned young, never lost — is embedded in everything he does professionally and in the community.

### The Captain America Exchange

There's a Captain America moment Aaron talks about. Before Steve Rogers gets the super serum, Dr. Abraham Erskine explains why he chose the small, weak kid:

**Steve:** "Why me?"

**Erskine:** "I'm not looking for a perfect soldier, but a good man. You see, the strong man who has known power all his life may lose respect for that power. But a weak man knows the value of strength. He knows the value of power."

**Erskine:** "Whatever happens tomorrow, you must promise me one thing. That you will stay who you are. Not a perfect soldier, but a good man."

**Steve:** "I'll do my best."

When Aaron closes his eyes, he can still see himself at 10 years old — the smallest kid in his class, coke bottle glasses, the last to be picked, someone who got picked on and lacked confidence. It drives him every day to work hard and do the right thing for others.

> "I more naturally relate to the underdog than the prom king. And I think that's made me more effective at everything — connecting, teaching, leading. Because the people who are the most receptive are always the ones who haven't been given very much yet."

## The COVID Community Story

During the COVID pandemic, when Governor Pritzker announced new restrictions that threatened Batavia's restaurants, Aaron and a group of neighbors organized to help.

> "COVID is on all of our minds and when Governor Pritzker announced new restrictions, we were saying we want to help restaurants because we know it's going to be really difficult for them to survive this winter."

One of Aaron's neighbors, Geoff Cheesman, had the idea to organize through their neighborhood texting group: once a week, pick a restaurant and encourage everyone to order carry-out that night. On one night alone, six families ordered from Riverside Pizza.

> "I started thinking, if this works, what if 25 or 50 neighborhoods across Batavia started doing something like this? That would have a real impact on some of these restaurants."

> "I also think that for the morale of those proprietors to know that — hey, we realize this stuff is going on and we're here to support you guys through thick and thin — I just think it would really have a huge benefit for everybody."

## FAQ: Honest Answers to Real Questions

**What do you actually do?**

Officially: Midwest Commercial Sales Manager for NIBCO, INC. — a national industrial manufacturer. What that actually means: Aaron helps MEP engineers specify the right systems for buildings, helps contractors win and execute the work, and trains the apprentices who install it — all across an 11-state territory. The short version: he builds partnerships and creates demand.

**Why would you send someone to a competitor?**

> "I've had a lot of people say, 'You're the first guy I've ever talked to who would actually send me to the competition.' My answer: why would I try to steer you to something that's not going to work?"

If Aaron doesn't have what you need, he'll tell you and point you to someone who does. That conversation gets remembered. That's more valuable than a transaction he shouldn't have made.

**How do you stay credible at every level?**

By showing up in both rooms. Aaron sits in on engineering design sessions to hear real challenges. He goes to union halls and works hands-on with apprentices. He's given national engineering presentations. He's also stood on job sites. Neither world respects someone who's just passing through. Aaron has done the work in both.

**What's your ideal business relationship?**

> "I always ask myself: I know why this is good for me. But why is this good for them? If it's not good for them, why would they want to do it?"

The ideal is alignment. Someone who understands how Aaron does what he does, trusts it, and wants to work that way.

**Why serve on a school board?**

His father Dean modeled a life of service — school boards, civic commissions, community organizations. Aaron ran for the BPS101 school board because his father spent a lifetime modeling what service looks like, and because he's passionate about making a positive contribution.

> "The work of children should not be political. My job is to make sure every single one of them has the foundations for success — wherever they end up."

**What's your leadership philosophy?**

Trust first. Everything else depends on it.

> "Disagreement and different perspectives are healthy — it's how things should work. But we should all be operating from a level of trust. If you have a concern, you should feel safe saying it to me. I can totally disagree with you and still respect you."

**Where do you struggle?**

Aaron is refreshingly direct about this:

> "Technology. Organization. I say yes to too much. I get worn down."

He goes to everything, takes every meeting, and ends up stretched thin across his professional, volunteer, and family life.

> "My wife says I'm not very efficient. And she's right. When I'm working at a high level, I do the small meaningful things — the handwritten note, the timely follow-up. When I'm worn down, those fall away. And that's when I'm not leveraging the work like I should."

**What's Aaron's real superpower?**

Genuine curiosity.

> "I actually want to know what you're doing. What your problems are. What you're trying to build."

That's not a technique — it's just who he is. And people can feel the difference.

> "You don't come across as a salesperson. You come across as a genuine human being who cares about other human beings."

That's not Aaron's assessment of himself — that's what people tell him.
